MSCS-Thesis

Overview

This thesis focuses on domain adaptation (DA) for malaria detection using microscopy images. It addresses challenges in medical imaging, such as the high cost and time required for annotation, and the performance drop due to domain shifts when applying models across datasets. The study evaluates Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) and Source-Free Domain Adaptation (SFDA) techniques on the M5-Malaria dataset, comprising high-cost (HCM) and low-cost (LCM) microscopic images. Experimental results highlight UDA methods like ConfMix performing well for HCM to LCM adaptations, while SFDA methods show promise but underperform in certain cases. Key challenges include data imbalance and visual differences between HCM and LCM images. The findings emphasize the potential of UDA and SFDA in enhancing model generalization for medical imaging tasks.

Abstract

Deep Learning models have shown remarkable success in medical image analysis tasks, such as segmentation and detection. However, these models often struggle with domain shifts between datasets, leading to performance degradation. This study focuses on domain adaptation techniques for malaria detection using microscopy images. This work evaluates Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) and Source-Free Domain Adaptation (SFDA) methods on the M5-Malaria dataset, which includes high-cost (HCM) and low-cost (LCM) microscopic images. Our results show that UDA methods,particularly ConfMix, perform well in bridging the domain gap, while SFDA methods show promise but struggle with specific dataset characteristics. Key challenges include data imbalance and visual disparities between HCM and LCM images. Our work highlights the critical role of domain adaptation in improving generalization and accuracy for unannotated datasets in medical imaging.

Dataset

For this study, we use the M5-Malaria dataset, which consists of high-cost (HCM) and low-cost (LCM) microscopic images. The dataset is publicly available and contains 27,558 images of malaria-infected and uninfected cells. The images are captured using different microscopes and imaging techniques, leading to domain shifts between the HCM and LCM datasets. The dataset is split into training, validation, and test sets, with annotations for the infected cells. The dataset is challenging due to the visual differences between the HCM and LCM images representation and the data imbalance between the classes.

A copy of used magnification is available at kaggle: M5-Malaria Modified. The original dataset is available at GitHub.
